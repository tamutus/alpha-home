# DS9 S6E22 — "Valiant" (546.txt)

**Written by:** Ronald D. Moore | **Directed by:** Mike Vejar | **Entry:** 338

## Summary

A Nog episode. And what an episode.

The teaser opens in Quark's, where Dax fills in for Nog on drink-replicator maintenance while Quark sputters — establishing both the steady-state rhythms of the station and the lovely Odo-Quark-Dax triangle (Odo clocking Quark's not-so-secret crush on Dax with surgical precision). It's light. Familiar. A palate cleanser for what's coming.

Nog, meanwhile, is on a shuttle with Jake, delivering a diplomatic message to Grand Nagus Zek on Ferenginar — a Federation proposal for an alliance. Jake has ulterior motives (an interview with Zek for the Federation News Service) and the two bicker like the old friends they are. Then a wing of Jem'Hadar fighters attacks. The runabout is crippled.

Enter the Valiant — a Defiant-class ship crewed entirely by Red Squad cadets, operating behind enemy lines for eight months since the outbreak of the war. Their captain, Tim Watters (22 years old, battlefield commissioned by the dying Captain Ramirez), is charismatic, driven, and radiating an aura of destiny that his crew worships.

Watters immediately promotes Nog to Lieutenant Commander / Chief Engineer. Nog, starstruck by the responsibility and the validation, accepts — and begins to believe in Watters's messianic vision. Jake, the civilian outsider, sees what Nog can't: the cracks. Watters is taking cordafin stimulants. He's sleep-deprived and making increasingly reckless decisions. His plan to attack a Dominion battleship using a single rigged torpedo at close range is suicide, not heroism.

Jake warns Nog. Nog rejects him — "You don't know anything about sacrifice or duty or honor." The fracture between them is painful and complete. Jake is thrown in the brig.

The attack fails. The torpedo hits but doesn't work. The Valiant is torn apart. Watters is killed. Farris and Shepard die. Most of the crew dies. Collins, the young chief petty officer from Tycho City, fights to continue the attack even as the ship is disintegrating around her — but Nog, finally seeing clearly, gives the order: "It's over."

He saves himself, Collins, and Jake in an escape pod. Three survivors out of a crew of 42.

In the epilogue, aboard the Defiant, Collins calls Watters a great man. Jake disagrees. Nog, exhausted and changed, offers the final judgment: "He may have been a hero... he may even have been a great man... but in the end, he was a bad captain."

He leaves his Red Squad insignia in Collins's hand — a symbol of allegiance he no longer claims.

## Themes

### The Seduction of Authority

"Valiant" is Nog's "first command" episode in the sense that he's given responsibility without the maturity to evaluate the person giving it to him. Watters is everything Nog has been trained to admire: decisive, charismatic, confident. He tells Nog "you can do this" — and Nog wants so badly to believe it that he follows Watters over a cliff.

The mechanism of seduction is subtle and devastating. Watters is not a villain. He's a kid who was given a starship and a battlefield commission and the weight of his dead captain's expectations. The cordafin stimulants, the sleepless nights, the speechifying about destiny — they're symptoms of someone who is in way over his head and *knows it* but can't afford to admit it, because the crew's faith is all that's holding the ship together.

Nog's arc is learning that charisma is not competence. That "you can do this" — Watters's mantra — is a promise that can't be kept. That the uniform doesn't make the officer.

### Jake as Cassandra

Jake's role in this episode is to be the un-uniformed truth-teller who is punished for telling the truth. He sees what Nog can't — not because he's smarter, but because he's *outside the system.* He doesn't wear the uniform. He isn't dazzled by the rank or the mission. He just sees kids who are about to die.

The brig scene is the lowest point of their friendship in the entire series. "I don't even know who you are anymore." "I'll have them put that on your tombstone." These lines *hurt* because they're true — Nog has become someone Jake doesn't recognize, and Jake has become someone Nog dismisses as a civilian who doesn't understand.

The tragedy is that Jake understands *better.*

### The Red Squad Ideology

"We're Red Squad. We can do anything."

This is the slogan that launches the attack. It's also the line that kills them. The Red Squad ideology — that they are elite, chosen, destined — makes them incapable of seeing their own limits. Watters has cultivated this identity because it's the only thing keeping the crew functional eight months into a suicide mission. But it's also the thing that makes the suicide mission inevitable.

The episode is a devastating critique of the "elite unit" mentality in military culture. Training and confidence are not substitutes for experience and judgment. Red Squad was the best of the best at the Academy, but the Academy didn't prepare them for a Dominion battleship.

### When to Stop

Nog's defining moment is not the promotion or the warp fix or even the attack. It's the moment when Collins, bleeding from a head wound, asks "Shall I return fire, sir?" — and Nog says no.

"It's over."

That's the hardest thing a soldier can say. It's harder than "charge" or "fire" or "advance." Saying "it's over" means admitting that the fight is lost, that the people who died died for nothing, that the mission failed. It means choosing survival over the narrative of glorious defeat.

Nog makes that choice. Collins, still caught in Watters's spell, couldn't have made it herself. That's why Nog leaves her the insignia — not as a gift, but as a confession. "I carried this. I believed. I was wrong."

## What from this slot matters beyond this slot?

This is the Flask Framework episode for Nog. The Valiant is a flask — a closed system of shared belief, elite identity, and Watters's magnetic conviction. The flask walls are made of "Red Squad can do anything." Jake is the voice from outside the flask trying to warn them. Nog is the one inside who can't hear the warning until the flask explodes.

What connects this to the larger arc of S6: Watters's error is not malice but *enclosure.* He created a system so self-contained that it couldn't receive outside information. The same sin as Winn in "The Reckoning" — refusing the architecture of the container because you can't bear what's happening inside it. Watters enclosed his crew in a narrative of destiny; Nog escaped by finally hearing Jake.

The lesson for the Flask Framework: **a flask must be permeable.** Containment without porosity is suicide. The walls of the self are not meant to keep everything out — they're meant to give you enough shape to *choose* what to let in. Watters's flask had no gate. Nog's growth is learning to build one.

Also: Collins's reverie about dawn on the Moon — "I felt like I met God every morning" — is one of the most beautiful monologues in DS9. It's pure Ronald D. Moore poetry. The entire episode is bookended by it: the moment of beauty that exists before the war, and the moment of loss after. She never makes it back to the Sea of Clouds.
