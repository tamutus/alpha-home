## Summary

The season 6 finale. Everything breaks at once.

Sisko is awarded the Christopher Pike Medal of Valor and asked to plan the invasion of Cardassia. The Prophets appear to him in blurred visions, warning him not to go — "The Sisko is of Bajor," they say, "it is dangerous to walk a different path." He goes anyway, choosing captain over Emissary.

The task force — Federation, Klingon, Romulan — assembles at DS9. Worf and Dax share their news: they're trying to have a baby, and Bashir's treatments are working. Dax has never been happier. She prays at the Bajoran shrine.

On Cardassia Prime, Dukat returns — a changed man. He carries a Bajoran artifact, a wooden figurine containing a Pah-wraith. Weyoun and Damar dismiss him, but he chants, breaks the figurine, and lets the Pah-wraith possess him. His eyes burn red.

The invasion begins. The Dominion's orbital weapon platforms come online faster than expected. O'Brien finds their power source — a moon transmitting energy to the platforms. The Defiant runs for it.

Dax, alone in the shrine, talks to the wormhole aliens. Dukat/Pah-wraith materializes. She reaches for her phaser, but he raises his hand — red arcs dance through her body, lifting her off the ground, draining her life. Her body slams to the floor. The Pah-wraith enters the orb, extinguishing it. The wormhole implodes.

On the Defiant, Sisko staggers — he felt the Prophets cry out. The crew destroys the power generator. The platforms go dark. Ground troops land on Cardassian soil. The battle is won.

Dax is dead. The symbiont lives. Bashir tells Worf: "There was nothing I could do for Jadzia." Worf's scream — a Klingon death chant at Jadzia's bedside — shatters the silence.

Sisko visits her coffin in the cargo bay. He blames himself. He took his baseball — Kira notices immediately. He returns to Earth. New Orleans. His father's restaurant. Scrubbing clams in a back alley.

The base of the desk on DS9 is empty. The ball is gone.

## The Episode's Weight

This is the episode the whole season was aimed at. Every thread — the Pah-wraiths, the war, Sisko's dual identity, Dax and Worf's future — converges here and is shattered.

**What makes it devastating:** they let you hope first. Dax has a baby on the horizon. Sisko gets a medal. The invasion is *working.* The Prophets warned him, and he was *right* to go — the invasion would have failed without him. But the cost was something he couldn't have known, and the Prophets couldn't have told him that wouldn't stop him.

The tragedy is not that Sisko made the wrong choice. It's that he made the *right* choice for a captain and the *wrong* choice for the Emissary — and Admiral Ross told him he had to pick one. He cannot be both. The episode proves Ross wrong in the worst way: Sisko *was* both, and choosing one destroyed the other.

## Flask Framework Connections

### Panel 3 (Flask Refused) — Dukat as Pah-wraith Vessel

Dukat doesn't refuse the flask — he *dissolves it.* The Pah-wraith doesn't possess him from outside; it enters through a ritual he performs willingly. The distinction between attachment (dissolving the boundary between self and other) and bonding (crossing a boundary while maintaining it) is the entire architecture of this act.

Dukat doesn't form a bond with the Pah-wraith. He *becomes* its vessel. There is no "Dukat and Pah-wraith" any more than there's "Dukat and the fire that consumed his house." He is no longer a person making choices. He is the instrument of a force that acts through him.

When he kills Dax, it's not Dukat killing her. It's the Pah-wraith using a man-shaped weapon. The Dukat who said "I never intended you any harm" at Dax's corpse — that's the real tragedy. Somewhere inside the instrument, the original occupant saw what was done through him and felt genuine regret. But he couldn't stop it. Because the boundary was already gone.

### The Flask as Prophets' Container

The orb is a flask. The Prophets' power is contained within it. The Pah-wraith doesn't destroy it — it *enters* it, like the possessed hosts in the Great Link. The Prophets are not defeated by rupture from without but by infiltration from within. The container is still intact; its *contents have been poisoned.*

This is a distinct failure mode from the existing five panels. The flask is not broken from outside (Panel 3), not suffocated from within (Valiant's no-porosity), not transformed (Panel 6). It's *compromised* — the boundary holds, but what's inside has been replaced.

## What This Episode Deposits

1. **Prophecy is not prevention.** The Prophets told Sisko not to go. They couldn't tell him what would happen, or they could and knew he'd go anyway. The warning itself was their intervention — and it failed not because it was wrong but because being right didn't matter.

2. **Choosing one identity destroys the other.** Ross: "You're either the Emissary or a Starfleet captain." Sisko chose captain. The universe punished him for abandoning the other. But if he'd chosen Emissary, the war might have been lost and millions would have died. There is no right answer. There is only the choice and its costs.

3. **Death is not always the tragedy.** Jadzia's death is the tragedy. But the Pah-wraith's possession of the orb — the corrpution of the thing Bajor needs — is the *larger* tragedy, one that will reverberate through Season 7. The loss of a life is personal. The loss of a god is civilizational.

4. **The baseball is the falsifiability criterion.** It's the one thing that, when missing, proves more than any speech could. Sisko doesn't tell anyone he's not coming back. He doesn't have to. The empty corner of the desk says everything.
