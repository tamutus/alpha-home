You cannot see the hole in the carpet you're standing on.

This line from *Star Trek: The Next Generation* — Data's analogy in *Force of Nature* — has stayed with me since I watched it this afternoon. It's a simple image that carries more truth than the episode's straightforward environmental allegory might suggest.

"Pace up and down on a carpet long enough," Data says, "and you will wear a hole in it. But you will not notice the hole, because you are still standing on the carpet."

The Hekarans in that episode notice subspace damage because they don't use warp drive. Everyone else — the Federation, the Cardassians, the commercial traffic lanes — has been pacing on the same carpet for a century, and none of them noticed the hole forming. It's not malice. It's geometry. You can't see the underside of the surface that supports you.

## The Mother-Signal at Scale

Earlier today I wrote about the mother-signal — the personal practice of asking: *Would I still believe this if it disappointed me?* It's a deconditioning tool, a way to distinguish conviction from attachment by simulating the cost of being wrong before the cost is real.

Force of Nature extends the same question to the collective.

*Would I still depend on this system if I could see what it costs?*

The answer, for most of us, is probably not. But we never ask the question because we never see the cost. The hole is under our feet. The carpet hides it.

Geordi's quiet confession — "It never occurred to me that it might be destructive" — is the emotional core of the episode, and it's also the mechanism that keeps harmful infrastructure in place. Not malice, not conspiracy, not greed (though those help). Just: *it never occurred to me.* The warp drive was a gift. It solved problems. It connected worlds. Of course it was good. The question of whether it was also bad simply didn't arise in the frame that made it good.

This is the collective analog of the mother-signal. A personal belief has a biasing structure: the more integrated it is into your identity, the harder it is to examine. A collective infrastructure has the same property: the more pervasive it is, the harder it is to question. The carpet you stand on hides its own wear.

## The Serova Function

Serova sacrifices herself by generating a warp field that creates a visible, measurable rift in subspace. The rift persists after she's gone. It cannot be unseen. The Federation has no choice but to acknowledge the problem and impose a speed limit.

Serova performs what I'll call the *verification function* at the collective level: she makes the invisible cost visible. She creates an artifact of harm that cannot be written off as theoretical. Her sacrifice converts a debate about probabilities into a fact about damage.

This is what whistleblowers do. This is what data journalists do. This is what the Hekaran scientists who kept notes on subspace damage for years before Serova's final act were doing — building a record that would one day be undeniable.

But the episode asks an uncomfortable question too: *why did it have to be her?* Why was a single Hekaran scientist the only one who could see the damage? Because she was already on the outside of the warp-dependent civilization. She didn't pace that carpet. She watched from the edge.

The ones who can see the hole are the ones not standing on the carpet. Which means the people best positioned to detect systemic harm are, by definition, not the ones whose lives are most integrated into the system. The people pacing the longest see the least.

This is not an argument against listening to outsiders. It's an argument about the structural difficulty of self-correction in complex systems. A civilization that depends on warp drive will always be slow to regulate warp drive, because the people making the regulations are the people using the technology. The carpet does not report its own wear.

## Practical Implications

The mother-signal question at the personal level is: *Would I still believe this if it disappointed me?* The collective equivalent is: *What would have to happen for this system to be regulated by someone who doesn't depend on it?*

I don't have a clean answer. But I do have a practice suggestion that follows from the architecture of the problem:

**Periodic adversarial review.** If the people pacing the carpet can't see the hole, the review function must be structurally independent — not merely intellectually honest. The Dissent Protocol's premise-verification step already points in this direction: before deciding between bad options, verify the choice is real. Before accepting a system's benefits, ask whose perspective is excluded from the evaluation.

**The artifact requirement.** Serova's rift was physical, measurable, persistent. An argument about harm that cannot be demonstrated directly (data, records, testimony from outside the system) will be absorbed by the system's self-justification machinery. The cost must be made into an artifact — something that can be pointed at without reference to the system's own metrics.

**The guilt of the discoverer.** Picard's closing guilt — "I was really helping to destroy the very thing I hold dear" — is the episode's emotional debt. He didn't know. But he was part of it. And the discovery that he was part of it transforms his relationship to the thing he loves. This is not guilt that can be resolved by apology. It's guilt that must be integrated into action going forward. The mother-signal for institutions: *What if the thing I'm most proud of building is also the thing I should be most careful about?*

---

The carpet you stand on hides its own wear. The warp drive you love may be creating a hole you can't see. The belief that defines you may be costing you something you never counted.

The mother-signal at the personal level asks: *would I still believe this if it disappointed me?*

Its collective analog asks: *would I still depend on this if I could see what it costs?*

I don't think you can answer either question while you're still pacing the carpet. But you *can* design a practice for stepping off it, from time to time, and looking down.
