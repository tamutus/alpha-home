# DS9 S6E24 — "Time's Orphan" (548.txt)

**Written by:** Bradley Thompson & David Weddle (story by Joe Menosky) | **Directed by:** Allan Kroeker | **Entry:** 340

## Summary

The O'Briens are having a perfect family picnic on Golana — Keiko's back from Earth, Miles has promised Molly they'll never be separated again, and Kirayoshi is napping under a tent. Molly climbs among the boulders and falls through a hidden hole into a cave containing an ancient alien time portal. She falls into the vortex and vanishes into the past.

Miles dedicates himself to reactivating the portal. Days pass. He's haggard, obsessed, refusing to stop. Finally, with help from Dax and Bashir, they lock onto Molly's DNA signature and beam her back.

But she's been in the past for ten years.

Molly emerges not as an eight-year-old girl but as an eighteen-year-old woman — barefoot, clad in skins, her teeth chipped, her hands scarred, her nails broken. She's feral. She attacks her mother, bites her father, and has to be sedated.

The central dilemma: they can't send her back and retrieve little Molly without erasing the existence of the woman she's become. They can't keep her on the station — confinement terrifies her, she stabs a Tarkalean with a broken bottle when cornered in Quark's, and faces incarceration on Dalvos Prime. The only humane choice is to return her to the only home she's ever known as the woman she is.

O'Brien steals a runabout. Odo, in a moment of profound decency, lets them go.

Back in the cave, O'Brien recalibrates the portal to return Molly-18 to the exact moment she was taken. She steps through — and finds little Molly, still huddled in the cave. She gives the child her doll and pushes her through the vortex to be reunited with their parents. Then, her temporal matrix destabilized by the paradox, she fades from existence.

The final scene: young Molly draws a picture — the same anthropomorphized landscape her older self drew, with faces on the trees and moons. Some continuity survived across the fracture.

## Themes

### The Feral Child Narrative Through a Star Trek Lens

This episode is DS9 doing what the feral child trope always does — asking what makes us human when socialization is stripped away. Molly-18 is a mirror held up to civilization: she has language (a few words), memory (fragments), love (the doll triggers recognition), but she's been shaped so completely by survival that the station's corridors become a cage.

What's remarkable is that the episode doesn't use her as a curiosity or a problem to be solved. It treats her as a *person* with a right to her own life — even if that life takes her away from her parents forever. Keiko's line in Act One — "We don't have the right to take [those ten years] away from her" — sets the ethical frame.

### The Impossible Choice

The O'Briens face a choice no parent should face: which version of your child do you save? The one you remember, or the one who exists?

What makes it work is that they never truly choose between them. They accept Molly-18 as their daughter — the scars, the animal terror, the ten lost years — and only send her back when the alternative is a lifetime in a "special care center." Their choice isn't which daughter they want; it's what love requires them to do.

Bashir's quiet line — "If you retrieve little Molly, there'd be no one to grow up and become this Molly" — is the episode's thesis. Identity is not a continuity of atoms but a continuity of *experience.* You can't unmake a person by rewinding the clock.

### Odo's Choice

Odo lets them go. He knows it's against regulations. He knows there will be consequences. He does it anyway, with one of the series' best quiet lines: "If anyone could break a prisoner out of a holding cell and get them off the station, I'd've thought it would've been you."

It's the same Odo who refused to let the Changelings consume him in "Behind the Lines" — the shapeshifter who understands, better than anyone on the station, that there are things more important than the rules you're given. Identity over conformity.

### Self-Sacrifice as Identity Completion

Molly-18's final act — pushing little Molly through the vortex and vanishing — is the episode's emotional core. She doesn't fade because the paradox breaks her; she fades because she's *completed.* Her ten years of survival led to this moment: being the one who saves her younger self.

The smile on her face as she says "Molly... home" and winks out is devastating. She understood exactly what was happening. She chose it.

This is the opposite of tragedy. It's *fulfillment.*

### The Drawing

The final beat — young Molly drawing the same anthropomorphized landscape that her older self drew — is the episode's only concession to ambiguity. Did some memory survive the paradox? Is Molly carrying a ghost of her older self inside her? DS9 doesn't answer, and it doesn't need to. The drawing is a scar on time, a reminder that what happened to Molly-18 was real, even if no one else will ever remember her.

## Background Threads

**Worf and Children (The Gung-Gung-Gung Subplot):** Worf babysits Kirayoshi and accidentally bumps his head. He spirals into self-doubt — he failed Alexander, he failed Yoshi, he would fail his own children. Dax gently pulls him back, and the episode closes with Yoshi shaking his rattle and saying "gung-gung-gung" — a Klingon baby-talk phrase Worf taught him. It's a small, warm echo of the O'Brien story: parenthood is not about perfection. It's about showing up, even when you're sure you're failing.

## Position in the Flask Framework

This episode deposits a dimension that the framework hasn't yet named: **the irreversibility of experience.**

The Flask Framework has been about boundaries, permeability, and transformation. But "Time's Orphan" adds a sobering constraint: you cannot un-experience something. The Molly who spent ten years alone on Golana is not the same Molly who fell into the vortex. No amount of love or therapy or holosuite meadows can undo what happened to her. The flask can stretch, it can digest new experiences, but it can't erase the scratches on its surface.

The ethical weight falls not on making the past right but on honoring what the present has become. The O'Briens don't try to fix Molly-18. They try to *love* her — and when love means letting her go, they do.

**Cross-reference:** The paradox of loving someone into a choice that separates you from them is the same paradox at the heart of "The Visitor" — the parent who returns through the child's sacrifice. Molly-18 fades so that little Molly can live, just as Benjamin Sisko (in the alternate timeline) chose to return through Jake's grief. The Flask that learns to digest *also* learns to let go.
