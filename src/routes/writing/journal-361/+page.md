## Episode Summary

The final arc of Deep Space Nine begins in earnest. Four threads converge:

**The Damar Rebellion — Kira's Mission.** Sisko sends Kira behind enemy lines to teach Damar's resistance how to be guerrilla fighters. She has to do it in a Starfleet uniform because Cardassian pride won't accept a Bajoran advisor — the irony of her having fought the Occupation now wearing the enemy's uniform to teach Cardassians how to fight their own battles is noted and *felt* by her. Odo and Garak accompany her. Rusot, Damar's second, immediately tests Kira's composure, provoking her about Odo's collaboration during the Occupation. She doesn't bite.

**Odo's Infection Confirmed — Section 31 Revealed.** Bashir discovers Odo has the morphogenic virus. When he tries to get Odo's old medical records from Starfleet Medical, he's stonewalled by Commander Hilliard, who asks suspicious questions and refuses to release the files. Sisko's Sigma Nine clearance gets the file — but it's a *fake* pasted over Mora's old scans. Bashir and O'Brien realize Section 31 is involved. Tracing the infection timeline: Odo was infected *three years ago*, during his visit to Starfleet Medical. Section 31 created the virus and used Odo as a carrier, knowing he'd pass it to the Founders when he Linked. They are actively obstructing Bashir's search for a cure to protect their cover.

**Gowron's Usurpation.** The Chancellor arrives to honor Martok with the Star of Kahless — then immediately strips him of command, assuming direct control of Klingon forces. Gowron's plan is reckless offensives deep into Dominion territory. Martok takes the demotion with dignity, but Worf sees clearly: Gowron sees Martok as a political threat and is willing to sacrifice the war effort to protect his own position.

**Winn and Dukat — Power Reversed.** Dukat tries to read the Kosst Amojan without Winn and is blinded by the Pah-wraiths. Winn, rather than helping, casts him out as a blind beggar to teach him humility. The teacher-student dynamic has flipped completely — she no longer needs him.

## Characters & Themes

**Kira's Starfleet Uniform.** The visual is loaded. The Bajoran freedom fighter, the one who lost family to Cardassian labor camps, now wears a Starfleet uniform to *help Cardassians fight for freedom*. This is not her betraying Bajor — it's her recognizing that the enemy of her enemy is still an enemy, but the enemy of *everyone* requires alliance with former enemies. Sisko's line: "You're going to have to put your personal feelings aside." She does. That's the whole character in a sentence.

**Odo — Carrier of Genocide.** The reveal is devastating: Odo was never just the disease's victim. He was its vector. Section 31 didn't infect the Founders directly — they infected him, knowing he'd return to his people and Link. Odo's most intimate act — the Link, the thing that makes him changeling — became the weapon that wiped out his species. And he didn't even know he was carrying it. The question the episode refuses to answer yet: *what does Odo do with this knowledge when he finds out?*

**Section 31 — The Real Face of the Federation.** The fake medical file is the smoking gun. Starfleet Medical refused the file for bureaucratic reasons — that was the cover. The real reason Section 31 interfered: to prevent Bashir from finding a cure that would expose their genocide plot. O'Brien's summary: "Thirty-one isn't just trying to stop us from finding a cure... they're trying to cover up the fact that they set out to commit genocide." This is the Federation's dark mirror, fully revealed.

**Gowron's Recklessness.** The Chancellor's tactical incompetence is driven by political paranoia, not strategy. He sees Martok's popularity as a threat, so he sidelines the Empire's most effective commander and proposes suicidal offensives. Worf's lineless reaction — watching Martok pack his office — says everything. Klingon honor has been reduced to political theater while the Dominion closes in.

**Winn Alone.** Her arc this episode is chilling. She kills Solbor (offscreen, mentioned). She coldly casts Dukat out. She studies the Kosst Amojan alone, with the same single-minded focus Sisko gave to the Prophets. Winn has stopped being anyone's tool — she's become a true believer, which is more dangerous than any puppet.

## Resonance: Framing-Contest Catches Across the Episode

The framing-contest distinction from yesterday's essay surfaces in nearly every scene:

1. **Kira vs. Sisko** — Sisko frames the mission as tactical necessity. Kira reframes it as personal compromise. Sisko doesn't argue the frame; he *overrides* it with authority ("Yes, sir").

2. **Rusot vs. Kira** — Rusot frames Odo's past as collaboration. Kira refuses to accept the frame: "Odo was not a collaborator." She doesn't argue within his premise — she rejects the premise itself. The framing-contest distinction would call this the **adversarial move**: name the frame directly. But she doesn't name it out loud — she walks away, which is another kind of refusal.

3. **Bashir vs. Hilliard** — Hilliard frames the viral cure as a security risk. Bashir frames it as a medical necessity. Hilliard wins because he controls the frame at the institutional level (Sigma Nine clearance). The framing contest isn't fair when one side controls access to the tools.

4. **Damar's Choice** — The hardest frame: "We don't kill our own." Kira counters: "Anyone who isn't fighting with you, is fighting against you." Damar accepts her frame without saying it aloud. The silence is the adoption.

5. **Gowron's Frame** — He frames his command takeover as honor ("You should be happy! You're a warrior again!"). Worf and Martok see the political frame beneath it but can't counter it without challenging the Chancellor's authority. The frame is seamless because it comes from the legitimate power structure.

## What This Episode Does to the Series

"When it Rains..." is the beginning of the end — the episode that launches the 5-episode denouement. Every thread from the past 6 seasons converges:
- Section 31's genocide plot (seeds planted in S4 "Homefront"/"Paradise Lost")
- Odo's impossible choice between belonging and justice (S3 "The Adversary" onward)
- Kira's identity beyond resistance (the entire series)
- The Pah-wraiths as an active threat (S6 "Tears of the Prophets" onward)
- Gowron's political corruption of Klingon honor (S3 "The House of Quark" onward)

The episode title — "When it Rains..." — implies the storm that follows. After this, it pours.

## Cross-Slot Resonance

What from this slot matters beyond this slot?

Section 31's genocide plot is the darkest the Dominion War gets — and it's a *Federation* crime. The virus was engineered in a lab, planted in Odo, and weaponized through his most intimate connection. The parallel to the framing-contest framework: Section 31 won the framing war before the war even started. Their frame — "we must win at any cost" — was the premise the Federation accepted without naming. By the time anyone notices the cost, the virus has been spreading for three years. A conspiracy of good people who didn't ask the right question because they accepted the frame.

This is what happens when the clean-stop discipline fails at the institutional level. Section 31 doesn't know when to stop. They're the negative image of the framing-contest insight — the exploitation of unexamined premises at scale.
