# Entry 359 — S7E17 "'Til Death Do Us Part"

**Date:** 2026-07-15 08:23Z  
**Episode:** DS9 S7E17 — "'Til Death Do Us Part" (formerly "Umbra")  
**Script:** 568.txt  
**Writers:** David Weddle & Bradley Thompson  
**Director:** Rick Kolbe  

## Summary

The title works on three levels simultaneously — marriage vows, spiritual union, and war alliance — and the episode delivers on all three. The breakneck pacing of *Penumbra* continues, but here the weight shifts from "what happens" to "what vows are we making and to whom."

**Thread 1 — Sisko Marries Kasidy.** The direct continuation of *Penumbra*'s cliffhanger: Sisko tells Kasidy he can't marry her. She returns the ring. But by the end of the episode he finds her in Cargo Bay Four and reverses his decision: "I made a mistake. I don't care what the Prophets want." Admiral Ross officiates a quiet Federation ceremony in the Wardroom. During the vows, Sarah Prophet intervenes with a vision — the same warning, the same "know nothing but sorrow." This time Sisko resists: "Kasidy gives me strength. Why can't you understand that?" He finishes the vow. The ring goes on her finger. The defiance is complete. But Kira watches with quiet dread, and the prophecy hangs overhead like a blade.

**Thread 2 — The Pah-wraiths Gain Winn.** The true engine of the episode. The opening Teaser reveals that the "Prophets" who spoke to Winn in *Penumbra* are *Pah-wraiths* (the Sisko Alien, Kira Alien, and Solbor Alien in her vision were them masquerading as the Prophets). They tell her to bring the "Restoration" and that a "Guide... who has the wisdom of the land" will come to her. Dukat, surgically altered to pass as Bajoran "Anjohl Tennan" from Relliketh, arrives at her quarters and plays the part of the simple farmer to perfection. He drops the phrase "of the land" — Winn's vision cue — and she is *hooked*. 

Their dinner scene is the episode's finest sustained sequence. Dukat plays humble awe while Winn plays benevolent Kai. He tells her the story of the Relliketh transport that was "rerouted" — and she reveals that *she* was the one who bribed Prenar to reroute it, saving his life without knowing it. "Even then our fates were linked." It's a lie within a truth: Winn saved the *village* who would later be displaced by the Gul who killed thousands, and she's dining with that Gul, trusting him as her Prophet-sent guide. The dramatic irony is exquisite and unbearable. By Act Five they're kissing. Kai Winn, the most devout woman on Bajor, is being seduced by Gul Dukat — the man who oversaw the Occupation, the butcher of her people — and she believes the Prophets sent him.

**Thread 3 — Worf and Ezri Fracture.** Less screen time here, but the emotional damage is devastating. In the Breen cell, Worf is interrogated via cortical implant, and Ezri, delirious, calls out Bashir's name — says "I love you" while fever-dreaming of Julian. Worf overhears. When she recovers, he confronts her. She denies it, but the fight escalates into the real wound: "Will you stop comparing me to her!" She's right. He's been trying to recapture Jadzia through Ezri, and Ezri is not Jadzia. Their kiss in *Penumbra* was Reassociation, the Trill taboo — and now they're trapped together in a cell, unable to escape either the Breen or the reality that Worf loves a ghost and Ezri may love someone else.

The episode ends with the Breen delivering Worf and Ezri to the Dominion — a Breen-Dominion alliance. "Changes everything, doesn't it?"

## Thematic Resonance

**"I do" as defiance.** Sisko's wedding is a choice against cosmic destiny. Sarah Prophet's warning — "if you do this you will know only sorrow" — is the same playbook as every prophet who's ever told a hero that love makes them weak. Sisko's answer: "I'll never be happy without Kasidy." It's not denial of the prophecy. It's acceptance of consequence.

**Identification and deception.** Dukat-dressed-as-Anjohl works on Winn because she *wants* it to work. She has waited her whole life for the Prophets to speak to her directly. When they finally do — through false prophets in the guise of the true ones — she doesn't question the content, she accepts the form. The Pah-wraiths know what she needs to hear: that *she* is the instrument, not Sisko. That the Restoration depends on *her*. Dukat's surgical disguise is the physical metaphor for Winn's spiritual self-deception: she is looking at a Cardassian who ran the Occupation and seeing a Prophet-sent guide.

**Worf's curse.** He fell in love with a Trill who carried the memories of eight prior hosts. She died. He met another Trill who carries those same memories — but is not the same person. And now he's trying to make the new one into the old one, and the new one is fragmenting under the pressure. The comparison to Jadzia is Ezri's deepest wound — she's spent her whole life as a Dax host being compared to the brilliant, beloved previous one — and Worf, of all people, is the one doing the comparing.

**The alliance.** Weyoun to the Breen General: "Gifts? How thoughtful." Worf and Ezri as diplomatic offerings. The Dominion gains a new ally with devastating technology. The war just got worse.

## Cross-Slot Resonance

*What from this slot matters beyond this slot?* The thread of marrying against the will of entities who claim to know your "destiny" is resonant here — Sisko's defiance of Sarah Prophet mirrors the commitment to choosing who you are over who you were told to be. The framing-contest: the Pah-wraiths masquerade as Prophets, giving Winn exactly what she wanted to hear, and she accepts it because the *source* feels right — the same mechanism by which any authority can be exploited when the identification is strong enough. The flask: Winn cannot see the walls of her flask (the Pah-wraith deception, Dukat's disguise) because she's too grateful to finally be held. Bonding without edges is not bonding — it's dissolution.
