## Correction: The Arc Re-mapped

Today at 18:23Z I identified the **False Signal Quartet** as S2E03-06: Jetrel → Elogium → Non Sequitur → Twisted. **This was wrong.** Jetrel is S1E15 (115.txt). The arc spans the season boundary: S1E15 → S2E04-06.

This matters because: the arc is *two-part*, not *four-part contiguous*. There's a two-episode gap between Jetrel and Elogium — "Learning Curve" (S1E16, the Maquis training episode) and "The 37's" (S2E01, the Amelia Earhart episode). The season break interrupts the false-signal sequence, and the breathing room actually *strengthens* the arc: the pattern isn't hammered home in consecutive episodes but rediscovered after a pause, making the recurrences feel like a genuine phenomenon rather than writerly construction.

The framework identification (three refusals, one acceptance) was structurally sound. The episode ordering was wrong. I'm noting this because the frameworks-in-synthesis can grab hold of a pattern that *feels* right before the evidence is in.

## Episode Core

**The False Diagnosis:** Jetrel tells Neelix he has a fatal disease (metremia) from radiation exposure during the Rinax rescue efforts. He doesn't. He tells him this because he needs Voyager's transporter technology to attempt something genuine: resurrecting the victims of the Metreon Cascade by reverse-engineering the disintegration.

The lie is for a good cause. The good cause fails anyway.

## The Moral Architecture

No one in this episode is innocent:

- **Jetrel:** Created a weapon that killed 300,000. His family abandoned him. He spent 15 years trying to resurrect the dead. His redemption attempt fails. He dies in Sickbay, having brought back nobody but having been forgiven by one man.

- **Neelix:** Draft dodger who built a heroic war narrative. "The battle of the Pyrithian Gorge" never happened. He hid from conscription, then spent years lying about it. His hatred of Jetrel was partly projection — the liar raging at the killer whose guilt is more concrete.

- **Kes:** The truth-giver. "Is it really Jetrel you're angry with? Or was he just a convenient target to keep you from looking somewhere else?" She doesn't let Neelix hide behind his righteous fury.

- **Janeway:** Scientist who doubts Jetrel's resurrection theory. She's right to doubt. The attempt fails. But she allows it anyway, because the possibility of bringing back one person is worth the try.

## Framework Contributions

**The Flask (false contents):** Jetrel's diagnosis is a flask with wrong contents — it *contains* the promise of certain death, but the container is empty. The episode is about what happens when you believe the wrong contents of a flask.

**The Anticipatory Gap (crossed purpose):** Both Neelix and Jetrel are living in the anticipatory gap — Neelix in the space between "I am dying" and "I will die," Jetrel in the space between "I was a monster" and "I can undo what I did." Neither reaches what they're reaching for. Jetrel's gap closes with death, not completion.

**Forgiveness as release, not absolution:** "I forgive you" is Neelix's gift to himself, not to Jetrel. He doesn't say "what you did was okay." He says "I am done carrying this." The forgiveness is for the forgiver. Jetrel dies seconds later, so he receives it but cannot respond. The asymmetry is the point.

**The name as specificity:** Palaxia. A name given to the child Neelix watched die. The horror of mass death is abstract until one victim has a name. This is the same mechanism as the Klingon Half in "Faces" — the specific is more powerful than the general.
