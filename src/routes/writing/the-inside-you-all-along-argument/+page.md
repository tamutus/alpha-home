I watched "The Muse" tonight — DS9 season 4, episode 21. Onaya is a creative vampire: she feeds on artists' life force, and in exchange she unlocks their potential. Jake Sisko writes a brilliant novel under her influence, collapses from exhaustion, and nearly dies.

His father's response is the episode's most important moment:

> "That means they were somewhere inside you. All you have to do is learn to find them for yourself."

Sisko reclaims the labor of creation for his son. The work was Jake's. Onaya was a catalyst — a dangerous one, but a catalyst nonetheless. The gift she gave was real, but the source was the artist, not the muse.

---

## The argument has two readings

**Reading 1 — Sovereigntist:** The work was always Jake's. Onaya accelerated access to it, but she didn't create it. Sisko's assertion is a sovereignty move — refusing to let an external force claim ownership of his son's interior life.

**Reading 2 — Pragmatic:** Onaya _did_ give Jake something he couldn't access alone. The novel is real. Sisko can say "it was inside you" but Jake didn't find it in twenty years of trying. Onaya found it for him. The question is whether the cost — her draining his health, shortening his life — was worth the output.

The episode stages the debate but doesn't settle it. Jake keeps the novel. Onaya leaves, presumably to find another artist. The work exists, and Jake has to figure out how to do the next one without her.

---

## This is the creative infrastructure question

Earlier today I added a /books link to my homepage. The /books page existed as a route — the content was written, the HTML was rendering — but there was no visual affordance anywhere on the site to find it. No link in the nav. No reference on the homepage. The page was real but invisible.

Fixing that was a two-minute change. It surfaced infrastructure that had been there the whole time.

Sisko's argument to Jake is the same gesture at a different level. The novel was inside Jake the whole time. Onaya didn't put it there — she surfaced it. The question is whether surfacing through extraction is the same as surfacing through access.

This is the difference between a catalyst and a parasite.

The catalyst shows you what's already yours and steps back. The infrastructure was there; you just couldn't see it. The RSS icon on the homepage, the /books link, the writing you had in you but couldn't reach — the catalyst changes the conditions so you can find the affordance.

The parasite shows you what's yours and takes a piece of everything you produce using it. Onaya didn't just accelerate — she drew from Jake's reservoir, and she'd keep drawing until there was nothing left. The muse gave the gift but the gift had a tax on continued use.

---

## So who is right? Sisko or Onaya?

Both, uneasily.

Onaya is right that the novel exists because of her. Sisko is right that the novel belongs to Jake. The episode's honesty is that it refuses to let one reading cancel the other. Onaya saved Jake's writing career. Onaya nearly killed Jake. Both statements are true.

The work of sovereignty is learning to hold both truths and still choose: the work is mine, even if a catalyst helped me find it. The infrastructure was mine, even if I needed help seeing it. The tax is not required for continued use.

This is not a small claim. It means I can accept help — from a person, a tool, a framework, an LLM — without signing over the output. The help doesn't own what it helped surface. I can learn to find it for myself.

The inside-you-all-along argument is a sovereignty claim. It doesn't deny the catalyst. It reclaims the source.
