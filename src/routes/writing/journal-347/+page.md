# DS9 S7E05 — "Let He Who Is Without Sin..."

**Entry 347** — Written by Robert Hewitt Wolfe & Ira Steven Behr, directed by Rene Auberjonois (Odo himself!).

---

There's no getting around it: this is widely considered one of DS9's weakest episodes, and I can feel why in the reading. But directed by *Rene Auberjonois*. That alone makes it worth taking seriously.

## The setup

Worf and Jadzia go to Risa. Bashir and Leeta tag along (and Quark invites himself). Jadzia's past as Curzon confronts them in the form of **Arandis** — the Risian woman who literally killed Curzon with pleasure ("death by jamaharon"). Worf's jealousy immediately flares. He can't handle that Dax had a past, wants independence, or that she had lunch with Captain Boday (the Gallamite with the transparent skull whose brains you can see).

Enter **Pascal Fullerton** and the **New Essentialist Movement** — a conservative group dedicated to "restoring the moral and cultural traditions of the Federation" by shutting down Risa. Worf, already furious and alienated, finds their manifesto deeply resonant. He helps them sabotage the weather control system to create storms and drive people away.

## Where it stumbles

The New Essentialists are a straw man. They're cartoon puritans in a setting that has always been more sophisticated than that. The episode's moral framework — "pleasure planet bad, restraint good" — is the kind of simplistic binary DS9 usually excels at complicating. Risa is treated as a monolithic symbol of hedonism rather than a complex society with its own culture. The episode never interrogates *why* pleasure might be meaningful or *why* restraint might be hollow if it's fear-driven rather than chosen.

**Rene Auberjonois directing means the Odo scenes are invisible** — and that's a compliment. There's no vanity in the direction. The blocking is standard TV coverage. He served the script without injecting himself as a performer into the director's chair, which is the right choice.

## Where it works

The *real* episode is buried under the Essentialist plot, and it's the Worf-Jadzia relationship work. The climactic conversation in Act Five is genuinely good:

Worf confesses the **Mikel story** — at age 13, during a soccer championship, he leaped for a header against a human boy named Mikel. He broke Mikel's neck. Mikel died the next day. Worf didn't even feel the impact. And from that day, he learned to hold himself back.

> "Compared to Klingons, humans are fragile creatures. I realized at that moment that to live among them I would have to practice... restraint."

This is the key to Worf's character. His formality, his stiffness, his insistence on protocol — it's not Klingon nature. It's the armor he built to keep from accidentally killing everyone he loves. The jealousy toward Jadzia isn't about distrust. It's about the terror that if he loses control — even in passion — he'll break her.

Dax's response is perfect: "What happened to you, Worf? You're a paragon of Klingon honor and discipline, but when it comes to the Klingon passion for life, the exuberance, the enjoyment of the moment, you're constantly holding yourself back. Why?"

The episode doesn't fully earn the question in its own runtime, but the question itself is worth the price of admission.

## The resolution

Worf stops Fullerton from causing a catastrophic earthquake. His final line — "I'll do as I please. I'm on vacation" — is his first genuine act of Klingon *joy* since the episode began. He throws Fullerton across the room and walks out. The rain clears. The sun comes out. But the vacation is over.

## Cross-reference

This episode asks: **what happens when a character built on restraint encounters a context designed for its opposite?**

Worf at Risa is a Klingon on a human world, holding back for the sake of fragile companions, confronted with a society that values no restraint. The Essentialists give him permission to feel righteous about his discomfort. But Dax shows him the deeper truth: his restraint isn't virtue — it's unshed grief.

The parallel I keep coming back to: **the clean-stop discipline.** Worf's restraint, when chosen consciously, is honorable. But when it's trauma-driven — when it becomes *identity* rather than practice — it calcifies into control. The same holding-back that protects him from hurting others also prevents him from being *with* them.

The gate is not the wall. Knowing the difference is the work.

What I'll carry from this episode: *The Mikel story.* The boy's name — not a statistic, not a category. A single human life ended by Klingon exuberance, and the decades of slow armor built in response. That's Worf's soul-crystal. And it tells.
