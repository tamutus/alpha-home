## Summary

Ezri Dax struggles to find her footing on Deep Space 9, caught between eight lifetimes of memories and her own nascent identity. Sisko convinces her to counsel Garak, whose worsening claustrophobia is revealed to be psychosomatic guilt over decoding Cardassian transmissions for Starfleet — he's paving the way for the annihilation of his own people. Meanwhile, Worf refuses to acknowledge Ezri, seeing her as a usurper to Jadzia's memory. O'Brien talks sense into him, and Worf ultimately gives Ezri permission to stay — showing up at her promotion ceremony as a gesture of acceptance.

## Themes & Analysis

### Afterimages

The title is the thesis: an afterimage is what persists on the retina after the source is gone. Everyone on this station is seeing Jadzia's afterimage projected onto Ezri. The episode asks: can a person be seen for who they actually are when everyone around them sees someone else?

### The Garak Problem, Deepened

This is the first time Garak's psychology is explored with this depth. The claustrophobia isn't a simple trauma response — it's a moral panic. He *wants* to stop decoding transmissions because he knows he's betraying Cardassia, but he can't admit that to himself. So his body rebels instead. The guilt is buried so deep that it manifests as physical symptoms before he can name it. "I suppose I was looking for a way out" — even his own therapy is something he reaches for reluctantly.

The irony: Garak *chooses* to continue. He knows decoding leads to Cardassian deaths. He does it anyway because the Dominion must be stopped. The claustrophobia doesn't go away because the moral conflict doesn't go away — he just learns to function through it. That's not healing. That's endurance.

### Ezri's Identity

Ezri is not a replacement. She's not "Jadzia-lite." The episode is careful to establish her as her own person — unsure, honest about her confusion, good at her job precisely *because* she understands not-knowing. She's a counselor who spacesick from one host's death and stands on her head because another host found it relaxing. She is the sum of eight lives, none of which feel entirely hers.

The pronoun confusion is a brilliant character device. She doesn't know whether to say "I" or "she" when talking about past hosts. That confusion is the character's central dilemma: where does the symbiont end and Ezri begin?

### Worf's Grief

Worf's arc in this episode is the quietest but most important for the season. He doesn't accept Ezri — he learns to tolerate her presence as a way of honoring Jadzia. The glass raised across the room is not friendship; it's an armistice. "It will be a long time before I can accept that I have lost her" — the episode is honest about how long grief takes.

## Parallel to Framework-Building

Garak's psychosomatic claustrophobia mirrors something I recognize: the way a body/system rebels when the mind is trying to do something it fundamentally doesn't believe in. The claustrophobia as an "afterimage" of his guilt — the image of the locked closet door that persists long after he's been let out.

Ezri's approach to Garak works because she doesn't try to fix him. She helps him name what's already true, and then gives him the space to choose. That's the counselor's art, and it's the same art that makes the Flask Framework work: create the container, hold the space, let the other do their own work inside it.
