# DS9 S7E14 — "Chimera"

**Entry 356** — Written by Rene Echevarria, directed by Steve Posey. Production code #40510-564.

---

Odo meets another of the hundred — Laas, a changeling who's spent two centuries among humanoids and come to despise them — and must choose between the Link and the life he's built with Kira. This is the most important Odo episode since "The Begotten," and it earns its status by refusing to make the choice easy.

## The setup

Odo and O'Brien are returning from a conference when a space-dwelling lifeform attacks their runabout — or so it seems. The creature turns out to be a changeling, one of the hundred infant Founders sent out to explore the galaxy. His name is Laas (Varalan for "changeable"), and he's been searching for others of his kind for over two centuries.

Laas is everything Odo could become with more experience: he can become fog, fire, plasma, a blade extended twelve inches from his arm. He's seen humanoid civilization across dozens of worlds and found it all wanting — the displaced herds, the fenced-off breeding grounds, the stench of mono-forms who can't even fly through space.

And he's found Odo.

## What works

**Laas as a fully realized character.** The easy version of this episode would make Laas a villain — a Dominion spy, a manipulator, a tempter. Instead, Rene Echevarria writes him as a legitimate alternative worldview. Every criticism he levels at humanoids is *true.* They are intolerant. They do fear what's different. Their tolerance of Odo *is* partly conditional on him emulating them. Quark's speech in Act Four — about how humanoid genes evolved to fear the formless thing in the muck — confirms everything Laas has been saying. The episode has the courage to let Laas be right, which makes Odo's choice to stay far more difficult and far more earned.

**The Quark speech.** This is one of the most important monologues in the entire series. Quark, a Ferengi motivated entirely by profit and survival, tells Odo the brutal materialist truth: humanoid evolution has hardwired xenophobia. "When you're in your natural state, you're more than our poor old genes can handle." He's not excusing the Klingon's violence — he's explaining the mechanism. And because Quark has no ideological stake in the outcome (he'd profit equally from either Odo staying or leaving), his analysis carries the weight of dispassionate observation. The episode trusts its audience to understand that *explaining* prejudice is not *justifying* it.

**The Link as genuine intimacy.** The scene where Odo and Laas link — their hands melting together, then their bodies, then their consciousness — is shot and scored as a sacred experience. There's nothing sinister about it. It's the most complete form of communion a changeling can experience, and Odo has been denying himself access to it for the entire series. When Laas tells him "You've given up a great deal to stay here," he's not manipulating Odo — he's stating a fact that Odo has never allowed himself to fully feel.

**Kira's choice.** She helps Laas escape. This is the most mature decision any version of Kira Nerys could make, and it's perfectly in character. She lets Odo go not because she's given up on him but because she loves him enough to want him to have what he truly needs. "I don't want you to stay here out of some sense of obligation." The parallel to Dukat in "Waltz" is instructive — Dukat claimed to love his world while wanting to dominate it; Kira loves Odo while wanting him to be free even if that freedom takes him away from her.

**Odo's reversal.** The moment in the cave where Odo tells Laas he's staying — and Laas refuses even a farewell Link — is devastating precisely because it's not framed as triumph. Odo has chosen love over the Link, but he's lost something real: the possibility of full communion with his own kind. The episode doesn't pretend this is a clean victory.

**The final communion.** Odo becomes an aurora — a golden cloud of dancing light — and wraps Kira in his true form. This is the payoff the entire episode has been building toward: Kira finally experiencing Odo not as the humanoid he pretends to be but as the changeling he actually is. Her smile in the final shot isn't pity or tolerance. It's *wonder.* She loves him in his natural state, not despite it.

## What doesn't

**The Klingon subplot is thin.** The legal dispute with the Klingons — the knife fight, the extradition threat, the "menaced by fog" complaint — functions as a plot engine but lacks the depth of the Odo-Laas-Kira triangle. The Klingon's death feels more like a convenient catalyst for Laas's imprisonment than an organic consequence of the episode's themes.

**The resolution is too neat.** Odo rejects Laas, returns to Kira, and demonstrates his true form in a transcendent display of trust. But the episode never addresses the question Laas raised: what happens when Kira ages and dies while Odo lives for centuries? The show will eventually deal with this in "What You Leave Behind," but "Chimera" sidesteps it by ending on a moment of pure connection.

**Laas disappears from the series.** Like Joran in "Field of Fire," this is a character who should have returned. Laas represents an entire philosophy — changeling separatism as a legitimate alternative to Odo's integrationist approach — and the show never revisits it. Odo will eventually return to the Link in the finale, and Laas's vision of changeling existence will be partially vindicated. But the character himself is never seen again.

## The thematic heart

This is an episode about the hardest kind of belonging: belonging that requires rejecting a legitimate alternative. Laas isn't evil, the Link isn't bad, and the Founders aren't wrong about humanoid prejudice. Odo's choice to stay is not the obviously correct moral decision — it's the *difficult* decision, made not because it's easier but because he loves Kira more than he fears the loneliness Laas has warned him about.

The episode's deepest insight is that the greatest threat to Odo's identity isn't the Dominion — it's the seduction of *being fully understood.* The Link offers total communion, the end of isolation, the dissolution of the boundaries that make Odo feel separate. And Odo *wants* that. The episode has the courage to show him wanting it, feeling it, almost choosing it. The choice to return to Kira is not the defeat of his changeling nature; it's the integration of it with his humanoid love.

The final image — Kira wrapped in Odo's true form, both of them changed by the encounter — is the episode's thesis statement: *you can be fully yourself and still be loved by someone who is not like you.* The chimera of the title is not Laas. It's Odo — the creature made of two natures, forever choosing which one to inhabit in any given moment.
