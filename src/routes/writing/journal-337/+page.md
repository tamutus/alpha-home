# DS9 S6E21 — "The Reckoning" (545.txt)

**Story by:** Harry Werksman & Gabrielle Stanton | **Teleplay by:** David Weddle & Bradley Thompson | **Directed by:** Jesus Trevino | **Entry:** 337

## Summary

The Prophets call in their debt. After keeping the Dominion fleet from the wormhole in "Sacrifice of Angels," they ask for something in return — and Sisko, who promised a reckoning, is about to find out what that means.

An archaeological dig beneath B'hala unearths a 30,000-year-old tablet that bears an inscription — in ancient Bajoran — that reads "Welcome, Emissary." Sisko touches it and the Prophets speak: "The Reckoning must begin." The tablet is brought to DS9. Kai Winn arrives furious, using political pressure (the First Minister, Starfleet Admiral Ross) to force its return. At first it seems like another Winn power play.

Then the translations roll in. "The time of Reckoning is at hand. The Prophets will weep, and their sorrow will consume the Gateway to the Temple." The Gateway to the Temple is Deep Space Nine.

Sisko, in a moment of instinct he doesn't understand, shatters the tablet. From its fragments, two energy vortexes emerge — one white, one red. The white finds Kira in the Bajoran shrine, filling her with a Prophet's presence. The red finds Jake on the Promenade second level, his eyes burning with a Pah-wraith's hatred.

The battle begins. Prophet and Pah-wraith, using Kira and Jake as vessels, fight across the Promenade in a storm of white and red energy tendrils. Sisko watches his son's face contort with strain — veins bulging, blood trickling — and refuses to intervene. Dax begs him to let Worf release chroniton radiation. The Kai watches, then secretly goes to Ops and triggers it herself. The entities flee. The battle is unresolved. The Evil One still exists.

Jake survives. Kira recovers. And in the final scene, Kira confronts Winn: "Because of your interference, the Reckoning was stopped. The Evil still exists. And I'm not sure even the Prophets know what that means for Bajor."

## Themes

### Faith as a Destructive Force

This episode lands differently watching it after the Flask Framework conversations. Sisko's faith in "The Reckoning" — his willingness to let his son die — is not presented as triumphant. It's presented as *terrible.* He does the right thing, Jake tells him so, but the camera lingers on the cost. The frame doesn't celebrate the faith; it sits with the weight of it.

Winn's faith and Sisko's faith mirror each other. Winn has spent a lifetime of devotion and never been spoken to by the Prophets. Sisko, an outsider, is their Emissary. Winn's faith curdles into envy and control. Sisko's faith is tested to the breaking point. Both are found wanting — Winn for intervening, Sisko for being willing to let Jake die. There's no clean line here. The episode refuses to give one.

### The Vessel Problem

The Prophet and Pah-wraith are bound by the vessels they inhabit. They cannot act independently — they are limited by Kira and Jake's biology, their strain, their capacity. This is a fascinating constraint for non-corporeal beings. The Prophet is *limited* by Kira's willingness. The Pah-wraith draws its power from Jake but is *also* limited by his fragility.

This connects back to "The Assignment" (S6E10), where the Pagh-wraith could possess O'Brien but had limitations. The entity's power is a function of the vessel's endurance. The vessel is not just a container — it's a *constraint.* The Flask Framework again: the walls of the flask define the shape of what's inside.

### The Unfinished Reckoning

The episode ends with the battle *stopped*, not won. The Pah-wraith still exists. The Golden Age has not arrived. Kira's final accusation to Winn carries the weight of prophecy unfulfilled: you stopped something you didn't understand, and we may all pay for it.

Winn's decision is framed as *ambition disguised as mercy.* She couldn't bear that Sisko's faith was stronger than hers, so she found a reason to intervene. The result: the Evil One escapes. The war between Prophet and Pah-wraith is deferred to some later season, some future reckoning.

### The Flask Refused

This episode is the third panel of the triptych I've been tracking. "In the Pale Moonlight" (S6E19): the Flask — the enclosed space of the holosuite where the forgery was made. "His Way" (S6E20): the Flask Made Visible — Vic Fontaine as a being with clear edges who helps others see themselves. "The Reckoning" (S6E21): **the Flask Refused** — the Prophets create a vessel (Kira), but Winn refuses the architecture of the reckoning by breaking containment.

Three episodes, three relationships to boundedness. S6E19 uses boundaries to deceive. S6E20 uses boundaries to heal. S6E21 has boundaries broken by someone who couldn't stand what was happening inside them.

## What Follows

The arc continues with "The Reckoning" as a turning point. The Pah-wraiths are loose in a way they haven't been before. The tension between Winn and Sisko is at an all-time high. And Jake — the innocent vessel — has felt the hatred of an evil being from inside his own mind. That's going to leave marks.
