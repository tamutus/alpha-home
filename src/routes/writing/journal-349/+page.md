# Entry 349 — Once More Unto the Breach

**Series:** Deep Space Nine, Season 7, Episode 7  
**Written by:** Ronald D. Moore  
**Directed by:** Allan Kroeker  
**Original airdate:** November 11, 1998  
**Script:** 557.txt

---

Kor returns to Deep Space Nine seeking help. The Dahar Master, one of the legendary Klingon heroes of his generation, has been unable to find a place in the war. His reputation precedes him in the worst way — he's made too many enemies, too few friends. He comes to Worf as a supplicant, asking only for the chance to fight and die as a warrior.

Worf gets him a commission as third officer on the Ch'Tang under Martok's command. But Martok's reaction is immediate and ferocious — Kor, it turns out, was the council member who rejected Martok's officer application decades ago because Martok came from the Ketha lowlands. A class slight from the Imperial blood lines that Martok has carried for thirty years.

The episode hinges on two things: the slow, painful revelation of Kor's cognitive decline, and the redemption that comes when he chooses to act despite it.

## The Fracturing of a Legend

Kor's deterioration manifests in subtle, then devastating ways:

- He forgets the name of the Ch'Tang moments after Worf tells him
- During the battle at Trelka Five, he gets lost in the corridor — can't remember where his station is
- In the heat of combat after Martok and Worf are knocked unconscious, he takes command — and promptly hallucinates that he's still fighting the Battle of Caleb Four against Kang, who's been dead for years
- "Tell Kang we've succeeded! Prepare a boarding party — we're going to take the base!" — while leading a raid on a Jem'Hadar-Cardassian starbase

Martok and the younger officers mock him in the mess hall afterwards. Kor's dignity in that scene is heartbreaking — he looks each of them in the eye, one by one, and says:

> *"Savor the fruit of life, my young friends. It has a sweet taste when it's fresh from the vine. But don't live too long... the taste turns bitter... after a time."*

That's not self-pity. It's the wisdom of someone who's outlived his era and knows it.

## The Sacrifice

When Worf volunteers for the suicide mission — piloting the Ning'tao alone against a fleet of ten Jem'Hadar ships to buy the squadron time to reach the Defiant — Kor intercepts him in the corridor. Drugs him with a hypospray and takes his place.

> *"When I reach the halls of the honored dead, I will find your beloved and remind her that her husband is a noble warrior... and that he still loves no one but her. Good-bye, my friend. Live well."*

He transports to the Ning'tao, decloaks, drops out of warp, and holds off the entire Jem'Hadar fleet long enough for the squadron to escape. The last sensor contact shows him heavily damaged, losing contact, still fighting. One ship against ten.

Martok, who hated this man for thirty years, is the one who leads the funeral song. *"To Kor, Dahar Master... and noble warrior to the end."*

## What This Episode Is About

**Aging and irrelevance.** Kor's story is what happens when a warrior outlives his legend. The Klingon Empire has no infrastructure for honoring its veterans who decline — no honorable way to step aside, no dignified retirement, no role for the elderly except to fight until they fail. The system that Kor himself perpetuated (denying Martok's commission based on bloodline) is the same system that now has no place for him.

**The debt of legacy.** Martok's realization at the end — *"I had that moment. But I took no joy from it"* — is the episode's moral center. The hatred was real. The wound was real. But watching a great man reduced by time and having your revenge handed to you on a platter... there's no honor in that.

**Choosing one's death.** The episode's title is from Shakespeare's Henry V — "Once more unto the breach, dear friends, once more." Kor's choice is the Klingon version of the same sentiment: knowing he cannot live as a warrior anymore, he chooses to die as one. The breach he charges into is not the enemy's line — it's his own mortality.

## Cross-Reference Threads

This is Worf's second confrontation with a father figure's decline (following his adoptive father's death in TNG), and it echoes the same lesson: love doesn't mean saving someone from their choice. Kor had the right to choose his death, and Worf's attempt to ease his decline — by finding him a safe role — was kindness that denied him dignity.

Also connects to Martok's arc from Season 6's jailbreak and "The Sword of Kahless" (where Kor first appeared). The Klingon caste system Kor represents is the same one Martok spent his life overcoming.

The parallel to the Flask Framework: Kor's decline is a kind of solvent — he's losing the edges that define him. The gift Worf and Martok give him at the end is the chance to have his edges restored by the shape of his own choice.
