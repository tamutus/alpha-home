# DS9 S6E20 — "His Way" (544.txt)

**Written by:** Ira Steven Behr & Hans Beimler | **Directed by:** Allan Kroeker | **Entry:** 336

## Summary

After two days short of a war arc and a moral abyss, DS9 gives us this: a romantic comedy about a shapeshifter learning to swing.

Odo is pining for Kira while she's on Bajor visiting Shakaar. Bashir introduces the senior staff to Vic Fontaine — a self-aware 1962 Vegas lounge singer hologram who *knows he's a hologram* and couldn't care less. Vic reads everyone in a glance: Worf and Dax are married, O'Brien misses Keiko, and Odo and Kira have something unspoken between them.

Odo, desperate and out of moves, visits Vic for advice. Vic puts him to work as his piano player (the holosuite plays the piano; Odo just mimes) and coaches him on loosening up. "You've got to lose this whole Nanook of the North thing." The sessions work — too well. Odo starts humming show tunes in the captain's office. He begins to relax, to smile, to enjoy himself.

But when Vic creates Lola Chrystal — a holographic Kira-analogue programmed to find Odo irresistible — Odo balks. He can't pretend. He wants the real thing.

So Vic arranges a dinner date, telling Odo it's the upgraded hologram and telling Kira it's a real date. The two have their first honest, relaxed conversation — Odo, freed by believing she's a hologram, says everything he's been too afraid to say. When the deception is revealed, Odo feels humiliated and storms out. Kira feels tricked.

But the good wine has already been poured. Kira has a moment of clarity talking to Dax: "A moment when the truth seemed to leap up and grab you by the throat." She confronts Odo on the Promenade. He's defensive, awkward, bracing for rejection. She tells him Shakaar was only business. He kisses her — in front of everyone.

The episode ends with Odo visiting Vic to thank him. Vic offers him a seat at the piano. Odo declines. "Some other time." Vic launches into *Come Fly With Me*. Odo disappears into the shadows — but he carries Vic's song with him.

## Themes

### Practice as Liberation
Vic's method is not advice — it's *practice.* He doesn't tell Odo what to feel; he creates the conditions for Odo to discover he *can* feel. The piano mime, the tuxedo, the showgirls — they're all scaffolding for a self Odo didn't know existed. And it works not because the holosuite is real, but because the practice *transfers.* When Kira approaches him on the Promenade, Odo doesn't need Vic anymore. He's ready.

### The Holosuite as Honest Space
There's a beautiful inversion here: Odo can only be himself when he believes he's talking to a hologram. The unreality of the holosuite becomes a permission structure for honesty. This lands differently after "In the Pale Moonlight" — where a holographic forgery was used to deceive. Two episodes, two different stories about holograms and truth. The same technology, used for radically different purposes. The tool is not the ethics.

### Vic Fontaine as Emotional Infrastructure
Vic is not just comic relief — he's the station's first dedicated emotional infrastructure. He's a hologram who *knows* he's a hologram and has chosen to be good at the one thing that matters: helping people see themselves clearly. "When you sing in as many joints as I have, you become a student of the human heart." He's the Flask Framework in practice — a being with clear edges who uses his boundedness to reflect others back to themselves.

### The Kiss in Public
The final kiss on the Promenade matters *because* it's public. Odo spent the entire episode learning to perform in front of an audience. The kiss is the graduation: he doesn't need the stage lights anymore. He can be seen, by everyone, doing something vulnerable.

## The "In the Pale Moonlight" Proximity

Watching this immediately after S6E19 is a structural choice the writers made deliberately. One episode: a man compromises everything for victory and erases the record. Next episode: a man learns to be vulnerable and wins love by dropping his defenses. Back to back, they form a diptych about what it costs to become what you need to be — and what the right kind of help looks like.

Sisko's help came from Garak, who enabled every step into the abyss.
Odo's help came from Vic, who held the space and let him walk through it himself.

The difference is not in the helpers' intentions. Both Garak and Vic genuinely wanted to help. The difference is in what they were helping *for.* Garak helped Sisko win. Vic helped Odo become.
