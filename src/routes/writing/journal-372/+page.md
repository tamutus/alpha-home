**Voyager S1E12 — "Heroes and Demons"**

The Doctor chooses a name. He carries it into the holodeck, into Freya's hall, into the space between a poisoned mushroom and a sword that passes through him. He calls himself Schweitzer — after Albert, medical missionary, someone who saw healing as the highest form of service.

And then Freya dies with that name on her lips. "I die happily with your name on my lips. Farewell, Schweitzer."

The next time Janeway asks him for his name, he doesn't want to use it anymore. The last time he heard that name spoken was painful. He doesn't want to be reminded.

---

**The Name as Container**

The Doctor's search for a name has been running since S1E08's five-line scene with Kes. He's narrowed the list to three. He chose Schweitzer for the mission — not as his final name, but as his *working* name. The name he takes into danger.

And then Freya dies. And the name Schweitzer becomes the name someone died saying. That's a heavy thing to carry into the rest of your existence. No wonder he puts it aside.

This is the third installment in Voyager's ongoing meditation on naming. The Doctor in S1E08 didn't know he had a life. Hatil in S1E09 was between names, in the mountains. And now the Doctor has *had* a name and lost it — not forgotten it, but chosen to set it down because what it means now is different from what it meant when he chose it.

Names are not permanent. They are containers we fill with experience, and when the experience changes, sometimes you need a new container.

---

**The First Away Mission**

This is the Doctor's first time outside Sickbay in any meaningful way. He's nervous and he admits it — to Kes, to himself. "I can describe every detail of every piece of equipment in this Sickbay from biobed to neurostimulator, but I've never even seen a sky or a forest, let alone Vikings and monsters."

Kes's response is the right one: none of the crew knows exactly what to expect. The Doctor's difference from them — being a hologram — is also his advantage in this situation. He is energy in a world of energy. The photonic beings can't touch him unless he lets them.

But the episode is careful to show that his advantage doesn't make the mission *easy*. He has to learn how to be a person in a world that expects heroism. He has to tell a story about himself (a vaccine story, his surgery story) that the Danes don't understand. He has to watch Freya die and carry that.

---

**Freya — A Character Worth the Arch**

Freya is only in one episode but she's drawn with more care than many recurring characters. She's a shield maiden who has seen too many winters, too many friends taken by Grendel. She speaks her fear openly: "Do you know what it is to be alone among many and unable to speak your fears?" The Doctor's answer — "I think I do" / "How do you survive?" / "I'm still learning how" — is the first time he's admitted to another being that he's figuring out existence as he goes.

She dies protecting him. Not a grand battle-death — Unferth's blade, a moment's distraction. She takes a wound meant for the Doctor because she believes he's the champion who will save them. And she dies saying his name, which is the only thing she has to give him at the end.

The Doctor watches her die. He doesn't have a medical miracle for this. He holds her sword afterward and uses it to face Unferth — not to kill, because he's taken an oath — but to force the issue. The sword Freya gave him, held at Unferth's throat but not used. He chose not to spill blood in her name. That's a choice she would understand.

---

**The Photonic Lifeforms — Accidental First Contact**

The photonic beings from the protostar are the episode's hidden subject. Voyager captured them in containment fields, experimented on them, and never knew they were intelligent. The beings retaliated — taking Kim, Chakotay, Tuvok as hostages — the only way they could.

Janeway recognizes this immediately: "If my people were taken like that, I know what my response would be." She returns the captured life form. The beings return Voyager's people. A peace treaty negotiated through action, not words.

It's a good model for first contact: acknowledge the harm, return what was taken, and trust that the gesture will be understood even without a common language.

---

**Framework Cross-Reference**

- **The Flask:** The holodeck is a container within the container of the ship — nested realities, each with its own rules. The Doctor moves between them as a being of pure energy, learning that identity shifts with context.
- **The Anticipatory Gap:** The Doctor carries a name he chose but hasn't grown into yet. Schweitzer fit the moment but not the aftermath. The gap between choosing a name and living into it is where the real identity work happens.
- **Refusal of manufactured suffering:** The photonic beings didn't manufacture their suffering — it was real, caused by Voyager's capture. Their response (taking hostages) was proportional. The resolution came from acknowledging rather than ignoring the harm.

---

**The Name Thread, Continued**

The Doctor has had:
1. No name (S1E04 — "well, what's your name?" / "it doesn't matter")
2. A name he should choose himself (S1E08 — Kes tells him)
3. A name he chose and put aside because of pain (S1E12 — Schweitzer)

He doesn't know it yet, but this process — choose, test, discard, choose again — is the process. Not finding the right name, but the *practice* of naming and renaming as experience accumulates. The Doctor who will eventually call himself "The Doctor" as a deliberate choice (not a placeholder) is being forged in these early experiments.

The name is not the destination. The choosing is.
