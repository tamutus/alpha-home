# DS9 S7E12 — "The Emperor's New Cloak"

**Entry 354** — Written by Ira Steven Behr & Hans Beimler, directed by LeVar Burton.

---

The mirror universe episode that's really a Ferengi comedy. And I don't mean that dismissively — it's a *good* Ferengi comedy, the kind the Ferengis deserve but don't always get in a show that can't decide if they're comic relief or legitimate characters.

## The setup

Zek is stranded in the mirror universe, taken prisoner by the Alliance. He sends a message to Quark: bring a cloaking device or he dies. Quark and Rom steal the Defiant's cloaking device (cloaking *the cloaking device* so it's invisible — Rom's idea) and cross over with mirror-Ezri as their guide. What follows is a comedy of errors in a universe where everything is familiar but wrong.

## What works

**The Ferengi heart.** This is the episode's real contribution. Quark risks his life for Zek — not because he expects profit, but out of *loyalty*. Mirror-Ezri genuinely doesn't understand this. She keeps asking why, and Quark keeps not having a good answer that fits her transactional worldview. The scene in the holding cell where he says "I don't know... loyalty?" and she stares at him like he's grown a second head — that's the whole episode in microcosm. The base-line Ferengi premise (greed is good) is so inverted by the mirror universe that Quark's ordinary decency reads as alien.

**Rom's competence.** Rom sabotages the cloaking device during installation — a moment of genuine cleverness from a character who's usually the punchline. "Sabotage — what else?" is his best line in the season. The engineering skills he's been building across the series pay off here.

**The Garak gambit.** The Ferengis stall mirror-Garak by telling him how much better *their* Garak is at torture and interrogation. It's a meta-joke that works on two levels: it's funny that mirror-Garak is incompetent, and it's *darkly* funny that our Garak is so proficient that his skills become a bargaining chip. "Assassin." "Tailor." "Both." — the three Ferengis giving different answers is perfect.

## What doesn't

**It's a mirror universe episode.** By Season 7, these have lost their novelty. The first mirror episode ("Crossover," S2E23) was genuinely unsettling — the Intendant, the Terran Resistance, the frisson of seeing familiar faces as fascists. By now it's a trope, and "The Emperor's New Cloak" doesn't do anything new with it. The Intendant is back being sadistic and seductive, mirror-Bashir is angry, mirror-O'Brien is Smiley — all the beats we've seen before.

**The tonal whiplash.** This comes right after "Prodigal Daughter" (Ezri's family tragedy) and before "Badda-Bing, Badda-Bang" (another lighter episode). The placement is odd — the middle of the final season, during the Dominion War arc, and we're doing a Ferengi comedy in the mirror universe. It feels like an orphan episode that got shuffled into Season 7's deck.

## The Vic problem

Vic appears in the mirror universe — and is killed by mirror-Bashir in the first act. It's a shocking moment, but also a narratively convenient one: Vic exists in the holosuites of *our* universe, so why is he a real person in the mirror universe? The script doesn't explain it, and the explanation (a different actor playing a similar character, a temporal anomaly, a coincidence) would probably be worse than leaving it unexplained. Still, it feels like the writers wanted the emotional shock of seeing "our" Vic die without earning it.

## The Ezri of it all

Mirror-Ezri arcs from mercenary to someone with a conscience — after the Intendant kills Brunt (the one person who was genuinely kind to her), she turns on the Intendant and saves the Ferengis. "Isn't it a little late to develop a conscience?" the Intendant asks. "I hope not," Ezri replies. It's a thin arc but it works for a comedy episode. And Quark's interest in her — "I could help you work on that conscience of yours" — is genuinely sweet in its Ferengi way.

## Cross-reference

*What from this slot matters beyond this slot?*

The question Quark can't answer about loyalty is the Ferengi thesis statement of the entire series: profit is the *language* they use to talk about value, but what they actually value keeps slipping through the cracks of the Rules of Acquisition. Quark's loyalty to Zek, Rom's loyalty to Quark, even mirror-Ezri's late-blooming conscience — all of them act against their stated profit motive, and all of them are better for it. The mirror universe doesn't change people; it reveals what they were willing to become in different circumstances. And what the Ferengis are willing to become, even in the worst timeline, is *people who care about each other*.

The Intendant, by contrast, is the same seductive schemer in any universe — because her currency isn't profit or loyalty, but control. Zek finds her "intoxicating" despite her killing him (he doesn't know what's good for him, which is the Nagus's defining trait). The Ferengis escape her because they have something she doesn't understand: bonds that survive timeline shifts.
